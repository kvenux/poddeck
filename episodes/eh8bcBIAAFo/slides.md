# The Design Process Was Treated as Gospel

<div class="mt-4 p-5 text-lg italic border-l-4 border-red-500 bg-red-50 bg-opacity-30">

"This design process that designers have been taught, we sort of treat it as gospel. That's basically dead."

</div>

<div class="text-sm opacity-60 mt-2">— Jenny Wen on her Berlin conference talk</div>

<div class="mt-6">

Jenny gave a talk at a conference in Berlin in September called "Don't Trust the Design Process" where she argued:

</div>

<div class="grid grid-cols-2 gap-4 mt-4">

<div class="p-4 rounded bg-red-50 border-l-4 border-red-400">

### What died
The diverge → converge → diverge → converge waterfall. Going off for weeks to do research, discovery, beautiful mocks — then handing off to engineering.

</div>

<div class="p-4 rounded bg-blue-50 border-l-4 border-blue-400">

### What killed it
Engineers can now spin off their "seven **Claudes**" and produce working features faster than designers can mock them. The forcing function is engineering speed, not a design manifesto.

</div>

</div>

<div class="mt-4 p-3 bg-yellow-50 border-l-4 border-yellow-500 text-sm">

Even that September talk already feels outdated to Jenny — especially after Opus and **Claude Code** adoption over the holiday break.

</div>

# Two Modes of Design Work

Design work is becoming "really stratified" into two modes that coexist:

**Mode 1: Execution & Support**
- Engineers build scrappy versions of ideas
- Designer consults, gives feedback, polishes
- Working directly in code for last-mile quality
- Pointing engineers to the design system

**Mode 2: Vision & Direction**
- No longer 2-year or 5-year vision decks
- Now 3-6 month prototypes that point teams in the right direction
- Critical because "if we're all working towards something that has one greater cause, it's much more efficient"

# Figma Is Still Alive (And That's Okay)

<div class="grid grid-cols-2 gap-6 mt-4">

<div class="p-4 rounded border-l-4 border-blue-500 bg-blue-50 bg-opacity-30">

### Where Figma still wins

- **Exploring 8-10 different options** at once — coding tools are "super linear," you invest in one direction
- Fine visual and interaction details — typography, micro-interactions
- Having a canvas to compare directions side by side
- "Having those in a canvas where you can just explore that specifically is still so so helpful"

</div>

<div class="p-4 rounded border-l-4 border-green-500 bg-green-50 bg-opacity-30">

### The interesting twist

Lenny observes: engineers are moving away from IDEs to terminals and agents. Jenny still uses VS Code for CSS tweaks because it's faster than telling an agent to change a hex value.

**Jenny's prediction:** "Maybe IDEs now become useful for designers and PMs and engineers have moved on."

</div>

</div>

<div class="mt-4 p-3 bg-yellow-50 border-l-4 border-yellow-500 text-sm">

As a former Figmate (yes, that's what they call themselves), Jenny acknowledges bias — but says the gap Figma fills for exploration is still real.

</div>

# Where Human Brains Stay Valuable

<div class="mt-4 p-5 text-lg italic border-l-4 border-blue-500 bg-blue-50 bg-opacity-30">

"At the end of the day, someone has to decide what is actually going to get built and what actually matters. Someone still needs to be accountable for the decision."

</div>

<div class="grid grid-cols-2 gap-4 mt-6">

<div class="p-4 rounded bg-orange-50 border-l-4 border-orange-400">

### The honest admission
Jenny thinks designers might be "holding on to that a little bit too much" — the idea that taste and judgment are uniquely human. AI's sense of taste will get better.

</div>

<div class="p-4 rounded bg-green-50 border-l-4 border-green-400">

### What remains human
The hardest parts of building software aren't building it — they're the disputes about what to include, the accountability for decisions, the social dynamics of teams.

</div>

</div>

<div class="mt-4 p-3 bg-purple-50 border-l-4 border-purple-500 text-sm">

Lenny's analogy: like radiology — AI might do the diagnosis, but a human is still needed to sign off because someone needs to be liable if they're wrong.

# Chat Won't Die — But UIs Will Come Back

<div class="text-sm opacity-60 mb-4">On the future of AI interfaces: it's both, not either/or</div>

<div class="grid grid-cols-2 gap-4 mt-4">

<div class="p-4 rounded bg-blue-50 border-l-4 border-blue-400">

### Why chat persists
Chat opened up "infinite ways to work with the model" — a new way to talk to computers that didn't exist before. It scales across all levels of intelligence (Kevin Weil's observation). WhatsApp/Telegram/SMS integrations were a huge unlock.

</div>

<div class="p-4 rounded bg-green-50 border-l-4 border-green-400">

### Why UIs return
Claude recently released interactive widgets — weather, stocks, questions — and "people still like to see UIs and touch them and click them." For specific tasks, direct UI is more efficient than typing.

</div>

</div>

<div class="mt-4 p-4 bg-orange-50 border-l-4 border-orange-500">

**Jenny's prediction:** More UIs will be generated by models on-the-fly rather than hand-coded by designers. The interface becomes dynamic — chat for open-ended interaction, generated UI for specific actions.

</div>

# From Director to IC — And Why That Matters

<div class="grid grid-cols-2 gap-6 mt-4">

<div class="p-4 rounded border-l-4 border-blue-500 bg-blue-50 bg-opacity-30">

### Jenny's path
- Director of Design at Figma (12-15 designers, multiple managers)
- Joined Anthropic as IC — deliberately
- Managed a team briefly, then back to full-time IC
- Self-described "reluctant manager"

</div>

<div class="p-4 rounded border-l-4 border-green-500 bg-green-50 bg-opacity-30">

### Why IC right now
- "The design process has changed so much in this past year"
- Picked up hard skills she wouldn't have gained as a manager
- Needed to be close to the work to understand the new reality
- "If I had not worked in this environment, I don't know if I would have totally understood it"

</div>

</div>

<div class="mt-4 p-4 bg-yellow-50 border-l-4 border-yellow-500">

**The advice for managers:** Jenny thinks design managers should take IC rotations — similar to how engineering orgs make new EMs pick up tasks for a few months before becoming full-time managers. "Design probably needs to do something similar."

</div>

# What Was Rustiest Going Back to IC?

<div class="mt-6 p-5 text-lg italic border-l-4 border-orange-500 bg-orange-50 bg-opacity-30">

"Actually like doing crits, you know, and just getting criticized."

</div>

<div class="text-sm opacity-60 mt-2">— Jenny on returning to IC design work</div>

<div class="mt-6">

The vulnerability of presenting work and receiving critical feedback on a regular basis. As a manager, you give feedback. As an IC, you receive it — and that muscle atrophies.

</div>

<div class="mt-4 p-4 bg-blue-50 border-l-4 border-blue-500">

**On the future of management:** Pure people management ("just somebody to sort of set you up, help you in your career, have one-on-ones") is fading. The future manager combines direction-setting with people management — someone who can "really engage with the team in terms of the work."

</div>

<div class="mt-3 p-3 bg-green-50 border-l-4 border-green-500 text-sm">

Her motto for team leadership: "whatever it takes" — whether the team needs direction, execution, or both.

</div>

# Three Hiring Archetypes

Jenny sees three types of designers that are especially valuable right now:

<div class="mt-3 p-3 bg-blue-50 border-l-4 border-blue-500 text-sm">

**1. Strong Generalist** — "block shaped" in the T-shaped framework. 80th percentile good at multiple skills. Design + PM + engineering shaped.

</div>

<div class="mt-2 p-3 bg-orange-50 border-l-4 border-orange-500 text-sm">

**2. Deep Specialist** — T-shaped but the tip goes "down farther than most." Top 10% in one area. Could be 50% software engineer, or incredible at visual design.

</div>

<div class="mt-2 p-3 bg-green-50 border-l-4 border-green-500 text-sm">

**3. Craft New Grad** — The one "we're all overlooking." Early career, wise beyond years, eager, humble, blank slate. No baked-in processes to unlearn.

</div>

# The Case for Craft New Grads

<div class="mt-4 p-5 text-lg italic border-l-4 border-green-500 bg-green-50 bg-opacity-30">

"Most companies are just hiring like senior talent... but given how much the roles are changing, having somebody who almost has like a blank slate and is just like a really quick learner... that's super valuable."

</div>

<div class="grid grid-cols-2 gap-4 mt-6">

<div class="p-4 rounded bg-green-50 border-l-4 border-green-400">

### Why they're special
- No baked-in processes or rituals to unlearn
- "Sometimes they're actually unburdened" by lack of experience
- They feel "anything is possible"
- They just build stuff and share it

</div>

<div class="p-4 rounded bg-blue-50 border-l-4 border-blue-400">

### Jenny's advice to young designers
- Build actual things — not theoretical exercises
- Find a community (she mentions Socratica — a school movement about building and showcasing projects)
- Don't feel limited by how little experience you have
- "The best crack new grad folks I know are just people who just use the technology, build actual things"

</div>

# Should Designers Learn to Code?

<div class="mt-6">

Jenny's nuanced take:

</div>

<div class="grid grid-cols-3 gap-4 mt-4">

<div class="p-4 rounded bg-green-50 border-2 border-green-200">

### Yes, somewhat
"More and more of the designer vocabulary right now is implementing some stuff." The tools are making it more accessible.

</div>

<div class="p-4 rounded bg-blue-50 border-2 border-blue-200">

### But not from scratch
You don't need to learn React from zero. "Start to bring that into your toolkit" — the coding tools, not necessarily deep technical knowledge.

</div>

<div class="p-4 rounded bg-purple-50 border-2 border-purple-200">

### The abstraction keeps rising
As models get better, "we will continue to move up the abstraction layers and you won't have to actually know how each single line of code will work."

</div>

</div>

<div class="mt-4 p-4 bg-orange-50 border-l-4 border-orange-500">

### Is Claude a hirable designer?
Jenny's honest answer: "I don't think Claude is there yet in terms of a designer you would hire." It's good at a first pass and presenting many options, but "nothing there quite feels like special and hirable yet." Good news for designers — for now.

</div>

# Low Leverage Is High Leverage

<div class="mt-4 p-5 text-lg italic border-l-4 border-orange-500 bg-orange-50 bg-opacity-30">

Jenny's "hot take": the low leverage stuff is often what has the most impact, because your team wouldn't expect you to spend time on it.

</div>

<div class="grid grid-cols-3 gap-4 mt-6">

<div class="p-4 rounded bg-blue-50 border-2 border-blue-200">

### Dog-fooding the product
Senior leaders who obsessively test, repro bugs, share logs with engineers, nitpick details — creating deep product familiarity that cascades.

</div>

<div class="p-4 rounded bg-green-50 border-2 border-green-200">

### Fixing bugs yourself
Jenny has seen Mike Krieger (Anthropic CPO) put in PRs himself. "It's really nice because it's like, okay, cool, we're all on this team together and nothing is below this person."

</div>

<div class="p-4 rounded bg-purple-50 border-2 border-purple-200">

### The anniversary card
Going out of your way to make someone's anniversary card or vibe-code something special. An EA could do it, but a leader doing it shows genuine care.

</div>

# Roasting = Psychological Safety

<div class="text-sm opacity-60 mb-4">Jenny's teams are known for being both happy and high-performing. Here's her secret:</div>

<div class="grid grid-cols-2 gap-6 mt-4">

<div class="p-4 rounded border-l-4 border-green-500 bg-green-50 bg-opacity-30">

### The roasting signal
When your team feels comfortable poking fun at you as a leader, it's a sign they don't fear you. Jenny's old team would imitate her saying "Okay, like what are next steps?" at crits.

The same comfort that lets you roast your friends — but applied to work. If it's there, the psychological safety is real.

</div>

<div class="p-4 rounded border-l-4 border-red-500 bg-red-50 bg-opacity-30">

### The balance: high standards
Psychological safety alone isn't enough. You also need high standards. Jenny frames it as "being a tough parent" — always there for the team, but pushing for the best work.

"Once you have that psychological safety... applying the high standards actually becomes potentially easier because you can do it without fear."

</div>

</div>

<div class="mt-4 p-3 bg-purple-50 border-l-4 border-purple-500 text-sm">

Lenny connects this to Radical Candor: caring deeply + challenging directly. Jenny agrees — that's the balance.

</div>

# The Legibility Framework

Jenny is obsessed with a 2×2 framework by Evan Tana (partner at SPC):

**Founders** can be legible or illegible.
**Ideas** can be legible or illegible.

The interesting zone: **illegible ideas** — ones on the frontier where people can't yet articulate the value.

Jenny applies this internally: scanning Slack prototypes, spotting energy around things she doesn't fully understand yet, then digging deeper.

<div class="mt-3 p-3 bg-green-50 border-l-4 border-green-500 text-sm">

"How can designers almost be more like VCs in this way internally — when we're looking at prototypes."

# From Illegible Idea to Co-work

<div class="text-sm opacity-60 mb-4">A concrete example of the legibility framework in action</div>

<div class="grid grid-cols-2 gap-4 mt-4">

<div class="p-4 rounded bg-blue-50 border-l-4 border-blue-400">

### The illegible prototype: "Claude Studio"
An internal prototype — "this really kind of like dense powerful interface" built on an agentic harness. It showed knowledge, skills, and previews of Claude's outputs. Jenny looked at it and thought: "I don't know what's going on. I don't really get it."

</div>

<div class="p-4 rounded bg-green-50 border-l-4 border-green-400">

### But there was energy
Research folks and internal builders gravitated toward it. Jenny's legibility antenna went off: "I think there's like something here, but I just don't understand it yet."

</div>

</div>

<div class="mt-4 p-4 bg-orange-50 border-l-4 border-orange-500">

### What came from it
The skills framework (markdown files that instruct Claude on how to do something) emerged from that prototype. When Jenny started working on Co-work, she pulled out patterns: showing Claude's plan and to-dos, the files it was going through — elements that users found genuinely helpful.

# Advice for Designers in an AI World

<div class="grid grid-cols-2 gap-6 mt-6">

<div class="p-4 rounded border-l-4 border-blue-500 bg-blue-50 bg-opacity-30">

### For new designers

- **Build actual things** — not theoretical exercises
- Use the technology, share with a community
- Check out movements like Socratica (building and showcasing projects)
- Don't feel limited by lack of experience — "they actually don't have expectations of themselves... they feel like anything is possible"

</div>

<div class="p-4 rounded border-l-4 border-orange-500 bg-orange-50 bg-opacity-30">

### For senior designers

- Bring coding tools into your toolkit (even if you don't learn React)
- The abstraction layers keep rising
- Resilience and adaptability matter more than mastery of old processes
- Try new methods, try new tools — "be really willing to adapt"

</div>

</div>

<div class="mt-4 p-4 bg-green-50 border-l-4 border-green-500">

**Jenny's shout-out to the Anthropic Design team:** "A team of folks that are really humble and they're not always loud on social but they're doing really great work... the team is so resilient." They span from deeply technical prototypers to high-craft production designers — and they're hiring.
